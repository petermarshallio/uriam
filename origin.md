# Uriam — Origin

The history of this framework: where it came from, what it's been called, and how it became what it is today. For what the framework actually is, see [`fundamentals.md`](./fundamentals.md). For strict definitions and tables, see [`reference.md`](./reference.md).

---

This framework was first developed in 1999, while working out the quality criteria for Keys4IT — Peter was barely twenty at the time. The original formulation was Learn, Think, Plan, Do — with a matrix identifying the source/target directionality of each stage.

Over the following twenty-seven years it has been renamed more than once (Spiral, previously CLAP, before that IDOFOBI/FOBI and LTPD, Perpetual Idea Machine), extended (bridges, gateways, a cast of characters), and applied to personal decisions, organisational change, and now AI system design.

One of those old names named a person, not a stage: a 2011 role called the "FOBI controller," whose job was to force a stalled group past its own overthinking and into a decision. It wasn't a precursor to the Stage Manager. It was the Stage Manager, caught in the one place its intervention is easiest to see — a group refusing to leave Think.

It was rediscovered and formalised in a single conversation on 2026-06-29 — which is itself a demonstration of the fractal property. The conversation was a Spiral cycle about Spiral.

It was renamed a second time on 2026-07-02, from CLAP to Spiral, once the stage names it had grown into — Learn, Think, Articulate, Build — no longer spelled the old acronym. Spiral turned out to be the more precise word regardless: not a flat spiral that grows outward, but a climbing one that keeps its shape and simply gains height.

On 2026-07-06, a second borrowed vocabulary got absorbed the same way: ArchiMate's Actor/Role distinction (a Role is a responsibility; an Actor, human or AI, gets assigned to one or more of them), and its Collaboration/Interaction pair. A full cycle turned out to already be an Interaction, performed jointly by whichever Actors hold which Roles — Notes and cues were already nested Interactions, just unnamed as such. The occasion was a practical question, not a theoretical one: how much authority should an AI hold over a human's own cues? The answer that fell out — none, unilaterally; authority is what a Notes Interaction produces jointly, not a possession of the Stage Manager Role — corrected an earlier draft of this framework, which had granted an AI in an AI↔Human flow more unilateral cue-calling authority than the rest of its own logic (bounded self-knowledge; no Actor sees the whole picture, least of all itself) actually supports.

On 2026-07-09, the matrix's own source/target logic was formalised into an explicit test (`reference.md`'s "Testing Whether an Act Belongs to a Quadrant"), prompted by a naming search for the fourth stage that kept producing words — Propagate, Cascade, Precipitate — that felt right for reasons the framework couldn't yet state precisely. Running the historical record through that test settled two things at once. First, the first and second stages each turn out to have kept a stable core across every era, once judged by what a word actually means rather than by its position in an old cycle's named transitions: the first stage absorbs CLAP v1's "Learning" cleanly, and the second absorbs both CLAP v1's "Planning" and CLAP v2's "Analyse" — the EESS-cue-derived positions, which had placed "Analysis" at the first stage and "Planning" at the second for reasons of transition-name-matching that had nothing to do with what either word actually means, don't survive being checked against the words' own definitions. Second, and more consequentially: every name this framework has ever given its fourth stage — Do (1999), Creating (2011), Create (2026-06-29), Build (2026-07-02) — fails the same test the same way. Each requires the actor's own current internal state as its origin, which makes all four Internal → External, not External → External. The fourth stage had been renamed four times in twenty-seven years and had never once had a name that actually described it.

None of the five tests arrived pre-formed; each one was pulled out of a specific example that broke something. The source test came from noticing that an artist painting from pure imagination has an internal source and an external target — the same shape as writing or speaking, not the shape of acting on something already out there — which is what exposed Do/Creating/Create/Build's shared error in the first place. The fractal trap came from asking whether "Planning" is really one stage at all: the mental sequencing behind a plan is internal-to-internal, committing it to a Gantt chart is internal-to-external, and a project manager's actual job — chasing a late vendor, reallocating a budget when a dependency breaks — is external-to-external, which means "planning" as a job title names all four stages at once, not one, and no amount of strict-definition sorting was ever going to settle it into a single box. The whose-spiral test came from a recommendation algorithm: it doesn't do anyone's internalising for them, it only manufactures the external conditions that make someone else's internalising more likely — a different act, belonging to a different Actor's spiral entirely, than the one being classified. And the Internal/External definition those tests all depend on came from asking what "internal" means for a camera with no memory versus one with a persistent store: internal turned out to be relative to whichever Actor's own spiral is being traced, not a fixed property of any medium — resolving a gap that had sat unnoticed in the matrix since 1999.

A second, independent technique came out of the same day's work, recorded in `reference.md`'s "The Phrase Test": embed a candidate word in the exact sentence the framework would actually produce — "before I internalise this, do I understand how it was [x]-ed by others?" — and listen for whether it reads naturally. This caught what the five tests alone couldn't. Precipitate failed on grammar: "was this precipitated by others" is stilted, because precipitate wants an event as its object, not information. Synthesize failed on connotation: it drags "synthetic" into a sentence where authenticity was the whole point. And Propagate's own adjacency to "propaganda" — which looked like a liability in isolation — turned out to be exactly right once actually said aloud: "was this propagated by others" is close to literally asking "was this propaganda?", the precise due-diligence question worth asking before internalising something.

Later the same day, the four stage names were renamed a third time, from Learn/Think/Articulate/Build to Internalising/Examining/Externalising/Interacting. The immediate cause was the fourth stage finally getting a name that passed its own test. The hunt for it tried and discarded Consolidating/Dissipating (Consolidating undersold the reasoning register the second stage needed, reading closer to gathering sheep into a field than to thought; Dissipating leaned entropic, implying decline when the fourth stage just as often means growth or amplification), then Affect/Effect (Effect nailed the fourth stage exactly — the plain English word for a consequence that isn't up to the person who caused it — but Affect undershot the reasoning angle, since psychology already defines affect in contrast to cognition, not as a form of it), then Evolving/Perturbing (astronomy's own term for change with zero agency in the loop, but "evolving" risks the reader hearing Darwinian selection instead, which is a fractal-trap word in its own right). Interacting resolved a gap that had sat unnamed in ordinary language the whole time — a frustration surfaced mid-search, that there was no everyday word for "everything happening outside the window" independent of anyone watching it — by reaching for physics' own term for exactly that: two external things acting on each other, the four fundamental interactions, no observer required anywhere in the definition. Once that name existed, the other three were brought into the same grammatical family — gerunds throughout, rather than the bare imperatives Learn/Think/Articulate/Build had been — partly for symmetry, and partly because Examining and Externalising turned out to be more precise than Think and Articulate had ever been: Think collided with ReAct's own use of the word, and Articulate carried an "explaining to someone" narrowness that Externalising doesn't (a candidate word, Explaining, was tried and rejected for the second stage for the mirror reason — it collides with Externalising's own territory more often than not). Internalising and Externalising themselves trace to developmental psychology's own internalizing/externalizing dichotomy for child behaviour, discovered independently and only afterward recognised as already-established terminology — the same pattern as the FOBI controller turning out to already be the Stage Manager.

Later the same day, once the fourth stage had a name that finally fit, the cast built around the old one stopped fitting too. The Producer had been named for a theatrical producer's feasibility work — budget, logistics, whether the thing could actually be mounted — but "produce" itself shares an origin with "produce" the verb, *producere*, to lead forth: the same creation-connotation error the fourth stage's own name had just been corrected for, missed on a first pass because the word was defended by its job rather than checked by its etymology. The question that actually broke it open was Peter's: *what can an Actor do, agentively, during External→External?* Working through it against the Interacting stage's own definition — an act with an external source can't originate in anyone's current internal state, or it's already Externalising again — the answer turned out to be: nothing. Anything that looks like doing during Interacting is a fresh Externalising act wearing Interacting's clothes; the only thing that actually happens inside the quadrant itself is watching what the world does, and choosing not to reach back in. Two independent technical analogies confirmed the shape from opposite directions: a monitoring system (Splunk watching a live system, using an external tool to observe an external effect, no comprehension required) and a CPU blocked on an interrupt (genuinely idle, executing nothing, until an external signal arrives) — the second one sharper than the first, because it distinguishes watching *well* from watching anxiously: a process polling in a tight loop is still, technically, "waiting," but it's burning cycles on intervention-readiness, not actually releasing control the way a true interrupt-driven wait does. That distinction produced the framework's own line for it: *the hard part of Witnessing isn't doing something well — it's tolerating not doing anything yet.* The Producer became **the Witness**. The Muse, unrelated to that discovery but flagged the same day as no longer sitting right once the theatre analogy was under this much scrutiny, became **the Dramaturg** — the cast member who interrogates a claim already held, rather than supplying inspiration for one that isn't. The Director and the Critic were checked against the same scrutiny and kept their names; both already described their corrected stages precisely. Renamed across `about.md`, `reference.md`, and `SKILL.md` — job descriptions and surrounding prose, not just labels — with one piece of unfinished business flagged rather than fixed: SKILL.md's own mapping of an AI agent's tool-use (writing a file, triggering a script) to Interacting turns out to have the same error the fourth stage's name always had, since an agent's own tool call still originates in its own current internal decision. That's Externalising, not Interacting, and is queued as its own fix rather than patched inline.

On 2026-07-19, the framework was renamed a third time, from Spiral to Uriam — Peter's own long-held domain, adopted deliberately as a backronym: Who U R and what I Am. The prompt was `bin/profile/primitives.md`, built as a stress test of how an AI actually understands the model — not asking it to run the four stages against a task, but asking it to classify itself as a Behavior Graph, classify a human the same way, and describe human/AI, human/human, and AI/AI collaboration in those same primitive terms. What that test surfaces isn't a mechanism being followed correctly, it's mutual recognition: an Actor working out who the other Actor actually is, and what it itself is, before any stage of the cycle can mean anything between them. The rename names that shift directly — the framework was never really about the stage mechanism in isolation, it's about two minds, or a mind and a machine, coming to understand each other's shape well enough to move well together.

Later the same day, the cast built around the old stage-matrix got re-examined against the bottom-up primitives that had just replaced it — not assumed to survive unchanged, per Peter's explicit flag. Two pieces didn't have a primitive to attach to. The Hero turned out to be redundant with a fact primitives.md already states for free — "a Behavior Graph is owned by an Actor" — so it was demoted from a Role with its own content to a plain deictic label, "whichever Actor is doing this right now." The Stage Manager's actual job — deciding *when* a crossing happens — had no Edge property to gate, since an Edge only relays, unchanged (`primitives.md`'s Edge Contract); the fix was recognising the job was never about the Edge at all, but about Role-assignment itself — since a Role is already defined as "a responsibility assigned to an Actor," the Stage Manager is just the responsibility of doing that assigning deliberately. Renamed **the Conductor**, both for the plainer fit and because it ties directly to the musical metaphor (unison/dissonance/counterpoint/suspension) already under separate consideration as the framework's primary relational framing.

That same conversation also caught something the 2026-07-09 rename had missed: the cast was never four separate Actors, always one Actor code-switching between Roles — already implicit in `reference.md`'s own Code-switching definition, just never reflected in the name "the Cast" itself, which implies several distinct performers. Renamed **the Repertoire**. Running the existing Phrase Test ("I am going to be a ___") against all five names then caught three more misfits the 2026-07-09 pass hadn't tested for, since it predated the correction. The Dramaturg failed outright — nobody says "I am going to be a dramaturg" — and was replaced by **the Philosopher**, chosen because Examining's own technique menu already spans both deduction (First Principles, 5 Whys) and interpretation (Hermeneutic Circle, Dialectical Reasoning), which a narrower word like Detective would have undersold. The Critic read too negatively for a Role whose actual scope is all of Internalising, not just post-hoc review, and was replaced by **the Student**. The Director undersold the hands-on-creation half of Externalising — the same error the fourth stage's own name had once made in reverse: Do/Creating/Create/Build were rejected in 2026-07-09 as names for *Interacting* because they require an internal source, but that's exactly what makes them correct words for *Externalising*, which the Director, a strategist who delegates the making, never actually claimed. Replaced by **the Maker**.

A new layer, **Persona**, was introduced below Role once it became clear a single name was doing too much work — Hercule Poirot's cold deduction and a more interpretive register are both genuine ways to occupy the Philosopher, not competing answers to who owns Examining. This is the same design principle `reference.md` already used for technique selection (a menu, not a mandate), applied one level up. Illustrative Personas settled the same day: Johnny 5 (*Short Circuit*) for the Student, Hercule Poirot for the Philosopher, Leonardo da Vinci for the Maker, Snoopy for Interacting's Role. Interacting's own Role name — "the Witness" going in — is the one piece left open rather than fixed, flagged for its own pass the same way the SKILL.md tool-use issue was flagged on 2026-07-09 rather than patched inline.

That first pass still had one Node owning exactly one Role, Persona nested underneath it — Peter caught that this didn't go far enough: an Actor has a Repertoire, not an assignment sheet, and "some roles fit better into some nodes than others" ruled out fixing even one Role per Node. The Role/Persona hierarchy was flattened as a result — Hercule Poirot isn't a variant of the Philosopher, he's a peer member of the same group, each bringing his own register to Examining's work — and each Node was rewritten as owning a *group* of Roles rather than a single resident, with membership by fit and degree, not exclusive assignment; a Role can belong to more than one Node's group. This directly answers the question TODO #7 opened: does the Cast survive the Uriam reframe? No — it's superseded by the Repertoire, and not as a rename alone.

Later the same day, work turned to TODO #8's first item: "Spiral" surviving only as an optional analogy, not the framework's name or a section built to defend the word. A false start first, worth recording so it isn't repeated — TODO #8's own note that the musical metaphor was "provisionally elevated to the primary relational framing" got read back as an agreed decision and nearly drove a restructure of `about.md` around it; Peter caught this immediately and flatly: he'd never said that, and TODO.md's own working note isn't the same thing as a decision made in conversation. Nothing about the musical metaphor's position was changed as a result — it stays exactly where and what it was. What did proceed, once separated from that error: a new page, `analogy-spiral.md`, absorbing every part of `about.md`, `SKILL.md`, and `reference.md` that used a spiral as the framework's image — the nautilus-versus-staircase argument, "spirals all the way down," the fractal property pictured as nested staircases. Every other document was stripped of the word entirely: "Spiral" as a proper noun became "Uriam" throughout, and "spiral" used loosely as a stand-in for "cycle" (a person's "own spiral," "whose spiral this is") became "cycle" outright. Left deliberately untouched: SKILL.md's registered `name: spiral` and its literal invocation phrases ("Spiral with me," "run Spiral") — changing the actual trigger without also resolving TODO #5's Cowork package resync risks breaking invocation, so that pairing was left for its own pass rather than patched piecemeal.

Still later the same day, `about.md`'s deletion — blocked earlier by what looked like a `.git/index.lock` issue — turned out to be a filesystem-mount permission problem instead; once cleared, the file was actually removed rather than left as a redirect stub. That prompted a second correction, the same shape as the first: Peter pointed out that a paragraph describing Momentum's skip-detection gap had been written straight into `fundamentals.md` and called "stated honestly" — his correction was that open questions must never appear in the text at all, only in TODO.md. Applying that rule consistently surfaced the same pattern already sitting in `reference.md`, `SKILL.md`, and `analogy-production.md` as inline hedges ("name under revision," "genuinely unresolved") — all pulled out and consolidated into a new TODO.md item. A related but distinct correction followed shortly after: describing a new Conductor technique table for `reference.md`, "calling the cue" slipped in — theatrical phrasing that belongs to `analogy-production.md` only. Non-analogy pages (`fundamentals.md`, `reference.md`, `SKILL.md`) describe things in primitives-grounded language (Node, Edge, Hop, Momentum); the vivid theatre/musical color is reserved for the two analogy pages. `reference.md` gained its fifth technique table as a result (Kanban WIP Limits, Timeboxing, Daily Standup, Incident Commander — the Conductor's own flow-control vocabulary, described by what each does to a Node crossing, not by "calling" anything).

TODO #5 (resyncing the installed Cowork "uriam" skill package) turned out to be a bad carryover from the pre-transfer note written before this project moved to Peter's personal account: there was no installed package in the new account to resync at all, just nothing installed — deleted rather than fixed. That cleared the last blocker on `SKILL.md`'s registered `name: spiral`, which was rewritten in full: renamed to `name: uriam` with matching invocation phrases, a real mechanism-level bug fixed (an agent's own tool execution — writing a file, triggering a script — was being labeled Interacting; by primitives.md's own source test it's Externalising, since the source is the agent's own current internal decision, not something already external), the same "calling the cue" phrasing stripped throughout, and its multi-voice review mode ("Convening the Repertoire") shrunk down to a pointer, with the full mechanics moved to a new `reference.md` section. The identical Interacting/tool-use bug was also found and fixed in `reference.md`'s own AI-system-design table — the same error, independently introduced in two places.

The framework has survived twenty-seven years not because it was written down carefully, but because it was *true enough to keep being useful.* `fundamentals.md` (originally `about.md`, split and renamed 2026-07-19) is the first attempt to write it down carefully.
